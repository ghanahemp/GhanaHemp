Hemp Fabric — How Hemp Becomes Clothing and Textiles

Quick Overview: Hemp transforms from plant to wearable fabric through harvesting, retting, breaking, hackling, spinning, and weaving. The result is one of the most sustainable and durable natural fabrics available today—and Ghana's climate and agricultural tradition make it an ideal crop for local textile production.

From Plant to Fiber: The Hemp Harvesting Process

Hemp clothing begins with the hemp plant (Cannabis sativa), which grows tall and fibrous. Unlike the cannabis plant grown for other purposes, textile hemp is bred to contain minimal THC (the psychoactive compound) and maximum fiber content. In Ghana, where agriculture remains a cornerstone of the economy, hemp can thrive in tropical and subtropical climates with minimal pesticide use—a significant advantage over conventional cotton farming.

The journey from plant to fabric starts with harvesting. Farmers cut mature hemp plants close to the ground, typically 70-90 days after planting. Timing is critical: harvest too early and the fibers are weak; harvest too late and they become brittle. The cut plants are then laid on the ground in rows—this is where the magic begins.

Understanding Retting: Nature's Processing Method

Retting is the most important step in hemp fiber production, and it's where many people are surprised to learn that nature does most of the work. Retting is simply the process of breaking down the plant's outer layers to expose the strong inner fibers. There are two main methods:

  • Water Retting: Hemp plants are soaked in water (rivers, tanks, or specially designed retting pools) for 1-2 weeks. Microorganisms in the water break down the pectin—a natural glue binding the fibers together. This method is faster but requires water management and can impact water quality if not done responsibly.
  • Dew Retting: Plants are left lying on the ground to be exposed to rain, sun, and dew over 4-6 weeks. Naturally occurring bacteria and fungi break down the pectin slowly. This method uses no water and is more environmentally friendly, though it requires careful weather timing and more land space.

For Ghana's textile sector, dew retting could be particularly advantageous given the country's seasonal rainfall patterns and the existing agricultural infrastructure. Water retting requires investment in retting facilities, which represents both opportunity and challenge for developing local hemp processing centers.

Key Facts: Hemp Retting

Water Retting Duration: 7-14 days | Dew Retting Duration: 28-42 days

Water Retting Temperature Ideal: 20-25°C | Cost: Water retting requires infrastructure; dew retting requires land and favorable weather

Breaking Down the Fibers: Mechanical Processing

After retting, the retted hemp stalks move to the next stages: breaking and scutching.

Breaking and Scutching

Breaking involves passing dried retted stalks through a machine with fluted rollers. This cracks and breaks the woody core (called "hurds") without damaging the fibers. The woody pieces fall away, leaving long, coarse fiber strands called "line fibers."

Scutching follows breaking. In this stage, the stalks are beaten or struck repeatedly to remove remaining woody material and impurities. The result is cleaner fiber bundles—but they're still not soft or uniform enough for fine textiles.

Hackling: Creating Uniform Fibers

Hackling is the final mechanical processing step. Long hemp fibers are combed through a series of progressively finer metal combs (hackles). This aligns the fibers, removes remaining short fiber fragments, and produces uniform, parallel fibers ready for spinning. The discarded short fibers—called "tow"—are valuable too; they're used in coarser fabrics and non-textile applications.

These mechanical steps are labor-intensive and require investment in machinery. They present opportunities for Ghana to develop regional processing centers, potentially creating jobs and adding value to locally grown hemp.

Spinning and Weaving: From Fibers to Fabric

Once hackling is complete, hemp fibers are ready for spinning—the process of twisting fibers together to create yarn.

The Spinning Process

Hackled fibers are fed into a spinning frame where rotating spindles twist them together, creating yarn of various thicknesses. The tighter the twist, the stronger the yarn. Hemp yarn is then wound onto bobbins or spools, ready for weaving or knitting.

Weaving into Fabric

Weaving interlaces two sets of yarn at right angles—the warp (lengthwise) and weft (crosswise)—on a loom. The way these yarns intersect determines the fabric's texture and weight. Plain weaves create smooth, lightweight fabrics; twill weaves produce diagonal patterns and heavier materials; satin weaves yield lustrous finishes.

Modern looms range from traditional handlooms (still used by artisans and in some Ghanaian textile communities) to industrial computerized machines that can produce thousands of meters daily. Ghana's textile heritage, particularly in regions with weaving traditions, positions the country well for hemp fabric production.

Ghana's Textile Opportunity: Ghana has existing expertise in cotton weaving and traditional kente cloth production. Transitioning some of this capacity to hemp could leverage existing skills while reducing the water footprint of the textile industry.

Properties of Hemp Fabric: Why It Matters

Hemp vs. Cotton vs. Linen

Understanding how hemp fabric compares to conventional alternatives helps explain why major fashion brands are adopting it.

Hemp fabric is approximately three times stronger than cotton, making it one of the most durable natural fibers for textiles.

Textile Exchange Industry Reports, 2023
  • Durability: Hemp outlasts both cotton and linen, with fibers that soften rather than deteriorate with washing and wear. A hemp shirt can last decades with proper care.
  • Water Usage: Hemp requires approximately 50% less water than cotton to produce equivalent fabric weight. In water-stressed regions like Ghana, this is significant.
  • Pesticide Use: Hemp grows vigorously with minimal pest pressure and typically requires no pesticides, unlike cotton which accounts for 16% of global insecticide use despite occupying only 2.5% of farmland.
  • Breathability: All three fibers breathe well, but hemp excels in moisture-wicking, making it ideal for hot climates like Ghana's.
  • Softness: Raw hemp fabric is coarse, but modern processing—including enzymatic treatments and multiple washing cycles—produces increasingly soft hemp textiles that rival linen.
