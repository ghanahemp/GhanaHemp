Hempcrete — The Building Material That Could Transform Ghanaian Construction

What Is Hempcrete?

Hempcrete is a bio-based composite building material made from three simple ingredients: hemp hurd (the woody core of the hemp plant stem), lime binder, and water. Unlike traditional concrete, which relies on cement and produces significant carbon emissions, hempcrete offers a sustainable alternative that is gaining recognition worldwide.

The hemp hurd—the fibrous, porous core left after the outer fibre is stripped away—makes up about 80-90% of the hempcrete mixture by volume. This material is lightweight, renewable, and has been used in construction for centuries in various forms. The lime binder (typically made from limestone) acts as the adhesive, while water activates the mixture. The result is a material that can be cast into blocks, sprayed onto frames, or poured into moulds, making it incredibly versatile for different construction methods.

Key Facts About Hempcrete
  • Made from 80-90% hemp hurd, lime binder, and water
  • Density: 300-500 kg/m³ (much lighter than concrete at 2,300 kg/m³)
  • Compressive strength: 0.5-3 MPa (lower than concrete, but sufficient for non-load-bearing walls)
  • Carbon footprint: negative to neutral (hemp absorbs CO₂ as it grows)
  • Fire rating: Class A1 (non-combustible)

Why Hempcrete Matters: Key Properties and Benefits

Exceptional Insulation

One of hempcrete's greatest strengths is its thermal insulation capacity. The porous nature of hemp hurd creates tiny air pockets that trap heat, providing an R-value (insulation rating) of approximately 0.28 per inch. This means a hempcrete wall just 30 cm thick can provide better insulation than traditional concrete blocks, reducing the need for additional insulation layers and lowering energy costs—a critical advantage in Ghana's warm climate where cooling costs are significant.

Breathability and Moisture Regulation

Unlike impermeable concrete, hempcrete is breathable. It allows water vapour to pass through walls naturally, preventing moisture buildup that can lead to mould and structural damage. This is particularly beneficial in Ghana's humid coastal regions and during the rainy season, as hempcrete helps maintain healthy indoor humidity levels without requiring mechanical dehumidification.

Carbon Negative Production

Hemp plants absorb carbon dioxide as they grow—one hectare of hemp sequesters up to 15 tonnes of CO₂ during its growing season. Since hempcrete is primarily composed of this plant material, the finished building material can actually be carbon negative when considering the entire lifecycle. This makes hempcrete an outstanding choice for Ghana's growing commitment to climate action and sustainable development.

Fire Resistance

Hempcrete achieves an A1 fire rating (the highest classification), meaning it is non-combustible and does not contribute to fire spread. The lime binder protects the hemp from ignition, making hempcrete safer than many conventional materials used in Ghanaian construction.

Pest and Mould Resistance

The alkaline lime environment created in hempcrete makes it inhospitable to insects, rodents, and mould. This is a significant advantage in Ghana's tropical climate, where pest damage and moisture-related deterioration are common problems in conventional buildings.

Key Takeaway: Hempcrete combines insulation, breathability, carbon storage, fire resistance, and pest resistance in a single material—properties that address many of the environmental and durability challenges faced by the Ghanaian construction industry.

How to Build With Hempcrete

Construction Methods

Hempcrete can be used in several ways, depending on project requirements and available equipment:

  • Formwork Systems: Hempcrete is mixed and poured into wooden or plastic forms around a structural frame (usually timber or steel). Once set, the forms are removed and the walls are left to cure.
  • Prefabricated Blocks: Hempcrete can be manufactured into blocks (similar to concrete blocks) that are stacked and mortared together on-site.
  • Spray Application: A wet hempcrete mixture can be sprayed onto a structural frame, creating a monolithic wall with no joints.
  • Infill Panels: Hempcrete is particularly well-suited to timber-frame construction, where it fills the cavities between wooden structural members.

Curing and Durability

Hempcrete requires longer curing times than concrete—typically 6-12 weeks for full strength development, depending on thickness and humidity. During this period, the material gradually absorbs CO₂ from the air (a process called carbonation), which strengthens the lime binder. Once cured, hempcrete is durable and can last 100+ years with minimal maintenance.

Ghana-Specific Considerations

Ghana's building codes and regulations currently do not specifically address hempcrete, which presents both a challenge and an opportunity. Builders interested in hempcrete projects may need to work with the Ghana National Building Authority to provide technical documentation and performance data. However, this also means there is significant room for innovation and the establishment of Ghana-specific best practices.
