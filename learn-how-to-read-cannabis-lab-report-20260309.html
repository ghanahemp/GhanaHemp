How to Read a Cannabis Lab Report — A Complete Guide

When you purchase cannabis in Ghana or anywhere else, the Certificate of Analysis (CoA) — also called a lab report — is your window into what you're actually buying. It tells you the strength, purity, safety, and chemical makeup of the product. But if you've never read one before, it can look like a foreign language filled with percentages, chemical names, and mysterious test results.

This guide will walk you through every section of a cannabis lab report, explain what each test means, and show you how to spot red flags. Whether you're a medical patient, a curious consumer, or simply interested in Ghana's emerging cannabis industry, understanding lab reports is essential for informed decisions.

What Is a Certificate of Analysis (CoA)?

A Certificate of Analysis is an official document issued by a third-party testing laboratory that confirms the chemical composition and safety profile of a cannabis product. Think of it as a detailed health report for cannabis — it tells you exactly what's in the product and whether it meets safety standards.

In Ghana, the National Cannabis Authority (NACOC) requires that licensed producers and distributors provide lab reports to ensure compliance with THC limits and safety standards. This means that any legitimate cannabis product in Ghana should come with a CoA that shows it has been independently tested.

Why This Matters: Lab reports protect you. They verify potency claims, confirm the absence of harmful contaminants, and ensure you know exactly what you're consuming.

The Main Components of a Lab Report

Cannabinoid Profile

The cannabinoid profile is the heart of any cannabis lab report. Cannabinoids are the active compounds in cannabis — the ones that produce effects and provide benefits. The most famous are THC (tetrahydrocannabinol) and CBD (cannabidiol), but there are over 100 known cannabinoids.

On a lab report, you'll typically see:

  • THC (Δ9-tetrahydrocannabinol): The psychoactive compound that produces the "high." This is displayed as a percentage (e.g., 18.5% THC).
  • THCA (Tetrahydrocannabinolic Acid): The raw, acidic form of THC found in fresh cannabis. It becomes THC when heated.
  • CBD (Cannabidiol): A non-intoxicating cannabinoid known for potential wellness benefits.
  • CBDA: The acidic precursor to CBD.
  • CBN, CBG, and others: Minor cannabinoids with various potential effects.

In Ghana, NACOC regulations specify that cannabis products must not exceed certain THC thresholds in order to be legal. Lab reports clearly state the total THC content, which is crucial for compliance. A product that exceeds Ghana's legal limit would fail testing and cannot be sold legally.

Terpene Profile

Terpenes are aromatic compounds that give cannabis its smell and flavor. They also contribute to the plant's effects — a concept called the "entourage effect," where cannabinoids and terpenes work together.

Common terpenes include:

  • Limonene: Citrus-scented; associated with uplifting mood.
  • Myrcene: Earthy, herbal; may promote relaxation.
  • Pinene: Pine-scented; may enhance alertness.
  • Linalool: Floral; commonly associated with calm.

Terpene percentages are usually small (under 1–2% each), but they significantly influence the overall experience. A product high in limonene will smell and feel different from one high in myrcene.

Pesticide Testing

Cannabis plants readily absorb chemicals from soil, water, and applied pesticides. Lab reports test for residues of common agricultural pesticides such as:

  • Fungicides
  • Insecticides
  • Herbicides

The report will show "PASS" or "FAIL" for pesticides. Any failure means the product should not be consumed. For Ghana's market, where agricultural practices vary widely, pesticide testing is essential for consumer safety.

Heavy Metals Testing

Cannabis can accumulate heavy metals like lead, cadmium, arsenic, and mercury from contaminated soil. These metals are toxic even in small amounts and can cause serious health problems over time.

Lab reports measure heavy metal concentrations and compare them against safety limits. A passing result means the product is safe to consume; a failing result indicates contamination.

Microbial Testing

Microbial tests check for harmful bacteria, mold, and fungi that could cause infections or health problems. Key organisms tested include:

  • E. coli
  • Salmonella
  • Aspergillus species
  • Yeast and mold

Products must pass microbial testing to be safe for consumption, especially for vulnerable populations like patients with compromised immune systems.

Residual Solvents

If a product is made using extraction (for oils, concentrates, or edibles), residual solvents — chemicals used to extract cannabinoids — may remain in the final product. Common extraction solvents include ethanol, butane, and CO2. While these are generally safe in trace amounts, high residual levels are dangerous.

Lab reports confirm that residual solvent levels fall within safe limits, or show a "PASS/FAIL" status.

Key Facts
  • A complete CoA tests at least 6 major categories: cannabinoids, terpenes, pesticides, heavy metals, microbials, and residual solvents.
  • THC content is the most strictly regulated aspect in Ghana.
  • All tests should show "PASS" — a single "FAIL" means the product failed quality control.

How to Find the THC Percentage

The THC percentage is usually featured prominently at the top of the lab report, often in a large, easy-to-read format. It represents the weight of THC per 100 grams of product.

Important notes on THC readings:

  • Total THC: This is THC + (THCA × 0.877). The 0.877 factor accounts for the weight lost when THCA converts to THC during heating.
  • Raw flower vs. decarboxylated: Raw cannabis flower will have high THCA and low THC. After heating (smoking, baking), THCA converts to THC.
  • Ghana's limits: Check Ghana's current NACOC guidelines for the legal THC threshold. Products exceeding this limit cannot be legally sold.
