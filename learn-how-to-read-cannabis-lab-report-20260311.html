How to Read a Cannabis Lab Report — A Complete Guide

If you're buying or using cannabis products in Ghana or anywhere else, you've likely encountered a Certificate of Analysis (CoA) — a lab report that tells you exactly what's in the product you're holding. But if you've stared at one and felt confused, you're not alone. Lab reports are full of numbers, scientific terms, and percentages that can seem overwhelming at first glance.

This guide will walk you through every section of a cannabis lab report, explain what each test means, and show you how to spot red flags. By the end, you'll be able to read a CoA with confidence and make informed decisions about the products you use.

What Is a Certificate of Analysis (CoA)?

A Certificate of Analysis is an official document issued by a third-party laboratory that has tested a cannabis product. It's similar to a nutritional label on food, but much more detailed. The CoA confirms what compounds are present in the product, in what amounts, and whether any harmful contaminants have been detected.

In Ghana, the National Narcotics Control Board (NACOC) requires all legally sold cannabis products to undergo compliance testing, including THC content verification. This is a critical safeguard for consumers and ensures products meet regulatory standards. A legitimate CoA should come from an accredited, independent laboratory — not from the company selling the product.

Why This Matters: A CoA is your proof that a product has been tested by an unbiased third party. Without one, you have no way of knowing what you're actually buying.

The Main Components of a Lab Report

1. Cannabinoid Profile

The cannabinoid profile is usually the first thing you'll see on a CoA. Cannabinoids are the active compounds in cannabis, and the most famous are THC (tetrahydrocannabinol) and CBD (cannabidiol).

The report will show percentages for:

  • THC: The psychoactive compound that produces a "high." This is the compound most closely regulated in Ghana.
  • CBD: A non-intoxicating compound often used for wellness purposes.
  • THCA and CBDA: The acidic forms of THC and CBD that convert to THC and CBD when heated.
  • Other minor cannabinoids: CBG, CBN, THCV, and others in smaller amounts.

Look for the "Total THC" percentage — this is what Ghana's NACOC compliance testing focuses on most heavily. For legal products in Ghana, this figure must meet regulatory thresholds.

2. Terpene Profile

Terpenes are aromatic compounds that give cannabis its distinctive smell and flavor. They also interact with cannabinoids to create what scientists call the "entourage effect" — meaning they may influence how the product affects you.

Common terpenes include:

  • Myrcene: Associated with earthy, herbal flavors
  • Limonene: Citrus scent and potential mood-lifting properties
  • Pinene: Pine aroma, potentially energizing
  • Caryophyllene: Spicy, peppery notes

Terpene percentages are typically very small — usually under 1% each. The overall terpene profile helps you understand what the product will smell and taste like, and may offer clues about its effects.

3. Pesticide Testing

Cannabis plants can absorb pesticides from soil and air, which then concentrate in the final product. The lab will test for common pesticides like pyrethrins, neem oil residues, and synthetic fungicides.

Look for one of two results:

  • "Not Detected" (ND): The best result — no harmful pesticides found above detectable limits.
  • Specific PPM readings: Parts per million (PPM) that should fall below regulatory limits.

If a report shows detected pesticides above safe limits, that's a major red flag.

4. Heavy Metals Testing

Cannabis can accumulate heavy metals like lead, cadmium, and arsenic from contaminated soil. These pose serious health risks with long-term exposure. Labs test for these and report results in PPM (parts per million) or micrograms.

All heavy metal results should show either "Not Detected" or readings well below established limits. This is especially important in Ghana, where soil quality and farming practices vary widely across regions.

5. Microbial Testing

Microbial tests check for harmful bacteria and fungi, including:

  • E. coli
  • Salmonella
  • Mold and mildew spores
  • Yeast contamination

Results should consistently show "Not Detected" for pathogens. Any positive result is a reason not to use the product.

6. Residual Solvent Testing

If a product has been made using extraction (like for oils or concentrates), labs test for leftover solvents — chemicals used to pull cannabinoids from plant material. Common solvents include butane, propane, ethanol, and hexane.

All residual solvent results should show "Not Detected" or be far below safety limits. Products made using safe, food-grade ethanol or CO2 extraction typically have cleaner solvent profiles.

Full-Spectrum vs. Isolate: Reading the Difference

A lab report can tell you whether a product is full-spectrum or an isolate just by looking at the cannabinoid and terpene profiles.

Full-Spectrum Products

Full-spectrum products contain multiple cannabinoids and terpenes. On the CoA, you'll see:

  • Several cannabinoids listed (THC, CBD, CBG, CBN, etc.)
  • A detailed terpene profile with many different compounds
  • A more complex, varied chemical fingerprint

Isolate Products

Isolates contain only one cannabinoid (usually CBD or THC). On the CoA, you'll see:

  • Only one or two cannabinoids present
  • Little to no terpenes
  • A simple, narrow chemical profile
  • Often higher purity percentages (95%+ of a single compound)
Key Facts
  • Full-spectrum products may offer more balanced effects due to the entourage effect
  • Isolates offer predictable, single-compound dosing
  • The lab report makes this distinction immediately obvious
